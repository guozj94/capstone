Process

How might we drive consumer preference for Mastercard?

More

Looked into various financial products, understand Mastercard B2B2C business model, identified current credit journey.

Synthesized interview results, and generated design ideas through brainstorming and co-design workshop.

Validated the concepts and user-tested prototypes with real target users and Mastercard internal stakeholders.

Research

We explored the world of credit from business, product, and technology aspects. We interviewed more than 20 people, conducted a co-design workshop, and synthesized using affinity diagrams, concept selection matrix, and personas.

Insights

“It’s an adult thing.”
"I know I have to learn, but I don’t know what I have to learn."

Younger generations are the market that has not been penetrated by credit cards. Credit card ownership is decreasing, and they don't see credit cards attractive at their age, nor have enough confidence managing them. Users tend not to have a preference between Mastercard and Visa since virtually all their customer experience and benefits are determined by the issuing banks.

Nearsightness
Young people don’t consider the value of building credit early in their lives. They lack credit literacy, thus don't fully understand the benefits
Fear of Credit
Young people have fear of credit. Not familiar with credit card terms and conditions, credit card fails to empower them, but becomes a risky borrowing tool.
Unmet Aspirations
Credit cards are a form of self-branding, and must support the story millennials want to tell about themselves. Uniformed credit cards are definitely less attractive.
Credit Continuity
As younger generations are about to experience rapid life changes, however, neither credit history nor credit card accounts support users through their various life changes.

Product Roadmap

Among all solutions, how do we better attract and retain a younger generation of credit card users?

In ideal case, universal profile builds the entire ecosystem that isn't currently existing, eases all users in, and makes Mastercard becomes their lifelong companion. However, requiring Mastercard to change their current B2B2C business model in near future is infeasible.

Even if we want to solve the problem at higher level and upper stream, we still keep the problem in a managable scope. Family plan boosts teenager's financial journey with parental guidance, fighting against fear of credit and helping them gain confidence.

Project Ohana

We want to educate teenagers to be financially savvy. Ohana fills a gap in the competitive landscape of credit training products.

Motivation Goal: Young users must understand the long-term benefits to use their credit cards responsibly.

Habituation Goal: Novice users must be trained to adopt healthy behaviors.

Basic Knowledge Goal: Novice users must learn basic terminology to apply these concepts in meaningful ways.

Next

Design

Ohana Journey

Level1: Beginner

Parents and teenagers get onboard, and teenagers use pre-paid debit cards but function like credit cards. Teenagers' credit scores will not be affected, and parents will have high level of control over the account.

Level2: Intermediate

Designed for teenagers with basic financial knowledge. Teens will have real credit cards and Ohana will protect their credit scores in various way. parents will have intermediate level of control over the account.

Level3: Advanced

Teens get full-fledged credit card. They will be financially literated enough to successfully manage their accounts. Ohana enables them to get more advanced cards that they wouldn't have access to otherwise.

Concept Validation

We validated the concepts using card sorting and storyboards with parents and teenagers. We listed the features that parents and teens would want, and asked them to cluster these features. We also tested the scenarios.

Prototyping & Evaluating

The whole Ohana experience includes teenager's card and mobile app, parent's app, and the bank's web portal. We prototyped all of them iteratively, and tested the usability with teenagers and college students.
